First things first:
what is perimenopause?

Perimenopause is the transition period before menopause. Your period is still there, but your hormones — especially estrogen and progesterone — start fluctuating. This can begin years before your last period, sometimes as early as your mid-30s.

On average, the first symptoms begin 4 to 10 years before the last period. Many women don't recognize the signs and spend years looking for other explanations — when the answer is often right there.

Why do so few women see it coming?

Because the symptoms come on gradually and often look like "normal" stress, fatigue, or a busy life. Many doctors miss perimenopause too — it's more complex than a simple blood test.

E Estrogen drops

Not linearly — it fluctuates. That's what makes symptoms unpredictable and variable.

P Progesterone drops first

Often the first hormone to shift, as early as your late 30s.

C Cortisol rises

The stress hormone becomes harder to regulate. Small stressors feel bigger.

T Testosterone shifts

Impacts energy, libido, and confidence. Often overlooked in standard care.

The 7 signs

Recognize several of these? Your body is probably trying to tell you something.

1

Irregular or changing periods

Your cycle becomes shorter, longer, or unpredictable. Or the flow changes — heavier, lighter, or just different from what you're used to. This is often the first visible sign that your hormonal cycle is shifting.

Note: irregular cycles can have other causes — worth getting checked.
2

Sleep problems

Trouble falling asleep, waking at 3am and not getting back to sleep, or restless nights in general. Progesterone has a calming effect — when it drops, sleep feels harder.

Tip: sleeping cooler and less screen time an hour before bed helps noticeably.
3

Hot flashes or night sweats

A sudden wave of heat from the chest upward, sometimes with intense sweating at night. Not everyone gets these — and the intensity varies enormously. It's the hypothalamus thermostat becoming less stable due to hormonal fluctuations.

4

Mood swings and irritability

Small things that used to roll off you now trigger a big reaction. Or you feel suddenly low without an obvious reason. Estrogen and progesterone both play a role in regulating mood via serotonin and GABA.

Often mistaken for burnout or depression.

5

Brain fog — trouble concentrating

Words you can't find, difficulty multitasking, a feeling that your brain is wrapped in cotton. Estrogen is essential for cognitive function — when it fluctuates, you feel it in your sharpness.

Many women think they're "getting dumb" — they're not. This is hormone-related and temporary.
6

Weight gain, especially belly fat

You haven't changed what you eat, but the scale is creeping up — and the fat seems to land at your belly now. Declining estrogen changes how fat is stored. Add cortisol and insulin resistance, and the picture makes sense.

More protein, fewer fast carbs, and strength training help directly here.
7

Dryness, low libido, or joint pain

Estrogen keeps membranes supple — when it drops, you feel it everywhere: vaginal dryness, dry eyes, dry skin, painful joints. Libido can drop due to a mix of testosterone changes, fatigue, and confidence.

These are physical symptoms, not psychological — and there are good solutions.

What can you do right now?

No big overhauls needed. These are proven, practical changes you can start today — and they actually work.

Adjust your nutrition

More protein (every meal), fewer fast sugars. This stabilizes blood sugar and helps with belly fat, sleep, and energy.

Omega-3 fatty acids

EPA and DHA support hormone production, reduce inflammation, and help with brain function. Choose a pure, concentrated form.

Stress reduction

Cortisol further disrupts hormone balance. Even 10 minutes of walking or breathing work per day has measurable effects.

Strength training

Muscle mass declines in perimenopause. Strength training 2-3x per week protects bones, improves insulin sensitivity and mood.

Protect your sleep

Consistent bedtime, cool and dark room, no alcohol before bed. Sleep is the single biggest lever for hormonal recovery.

Magnesium

Supports sleep, muscle recovery, and stress response. Many women are deficient without knowing. Glycinate or malate work well.

Want to know what your body specifically needs? Every woman is different. What works for someone else won't automatically work for you. A targeted approach — based on your symptoms, lifestyle, and optional testing — makes the real difference.
